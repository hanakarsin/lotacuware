Kenya's first attorney general, Charles Mugane Njonjo, has dismissed his death rumours.

On Saturday, October 9, reports emerged on social media claiming Njonjo, alias the Duke of Kabeteshire, had died.

However, on the morning of Sunday, October 10, his family, in a statement, dismissed the reports terming them as a great exaggeration.

According to the release, the 101-year-old is alive and enjoying his weekend at home.

“Reports of my death have been greatly exaggerated. I am well and enjoying my weekend at home as we celebrate Utamaduni Day,” read the statement.

Njonjo remains a special part of the country's history.

He was born on January 23, 1920, and he is one of the few people of his generation still alive, strong and kicking.

After his primary education, he joined Alliance High School and proceeded to the University at Fort Hare in South Africa for a law degree.

Nicknamed the Duke of Kabeteshire for his love of English mannerism, he served as Kenya’s AG between 1963 and 1979.

He resigned in 1980 to contest for Kikuyu parliamentary seat where he won unopposed.

'Sir Charles' another of his moniker, was also one of the defenders of the Jomo Kenyatta regime and also helped in the transitional government after Kenyatta passed on in 1978.

Marriage

Njonjo married a British woman, Margaret Bryson, at the age of 52 following alleged pressure from Kenya's first president who told him he could not get advisory from an unmarried man.

He, however, fell from grace after the 1982 coup after he was suspected of being part of the group that wanted to overthrow retired president Daniel Moi.

Njonjo resigned from his Cabinet and parliamentary position after the coup and retreated to a low-profile life.

Though he has been back and forth to the limelight, he is still an influential person.

He is known for his signature black pinstriped suits that he buys from London.

In a past interview, the only surviving member of the country’s first independence Cabinet said his grandchildren were his greatest source of joy.
