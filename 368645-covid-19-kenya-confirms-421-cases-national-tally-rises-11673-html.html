COVID-19: Health CS Mutahi Kagwe tells Kenyans seeking coronavirus tests to avoid Lancet lab

 Posted on May 3, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  517 words  |  Lourie Helzer

- Kenya on Thursday, July 16, announced the highest number of recoveries in a single day at 570

- Health CS Mutahi Kagwe said 512 of the patients recovered while undergoing home-based care while 58 recovered after treatment in various hospital

- The CS has urged people seeking COVID-19 tests to avoid laboratories that have been producing conflicting results like Lancet

The Ministry of Health has announced the highest number of patients who have recovered from COVID-19 in a single day at 570 pushing the country's tally of recoveries to 3,638.

Speaking in Nakuru county on Thursday, July 16, Health Cabinet Secretary (CS) Mutahi Kagwe said 512 of the patients who recovered underwent home-based care while 58 received treatment from various hospitals.

Kagwe further revealed some 421 people had tested positive for coronavirus after examination of 3,895 samples.

This pushed the country's count of the disease to 11,637 from 11,252 recorded on Wednesday, July 15.

The death toll increased from 209 to 217 after eight more patients died from the disease.

When questioned on why Lancet laboratory had frequent incidents of discordant COVID-19 tests, Kagwe said the government was still investigating the development but urged citizens to avoid seeking tests from institutions with such controversies.

"The ministry is going to take its measures. We have asked our board to review it. However, if you have been hearing that a lab has this unclear results, don't go there. Why should you go to a place that has been giving unclear information? As a government, we have a responsibility to check all the labs. After investigations...if it emerges a lab is not in conformity with regulations, then we will definitely close them down," said Kagwe.

TUKO.co,ke earlier reported that Lancet Kenya Laboratory was put on the spot over conflicting COVID-19 test results of 17 teaching staff of St Andrew’s School Turi who had tested positive only to test negative of the virus three days later.

The lab's Managing Director Dr Ahmed Kalebi, however, defended the tests stating their equipment was very sensitive to even the smallest traces of the virus.

Kalebi urged those who turned positive at Lancet but later test negative elsewhere should not ignore the Lancet results.

“Actually, there have been cases of people disputing our positive results, getting tested elsewhere as negative then ignoring our result, only for them or their relatives, close contacts to end up very sick a few days later – some have even ended up in ICU or died,” he said.
