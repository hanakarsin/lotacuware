The iconic Windows XP default desktop wallpaper of a sloped green hill beneath a bright blue sky is one of the most popular images on the internet.

But its generic pleasantness has perplexed internet users for a long time in regards to its real-world location, with some believing it is not a real photograph.

The editor-in-chief of SF Gate Grant Marek recently set out to find the earthly subject of the computer background and discovered it covered in wine grapes across the street from an alpaca farm and Highway 12 Sonoma, California.

According to him, the photo even has an incredible backstory. While driving to see his now-wife on a Friday afternoon in January 1996, Charles O'Rear snapped the now-legendary shot of what is known as "Bliss" Hill.

“A majority of people who saw that photograph, billions of people, thought it was not a real photograph,” O’Rear told Marek.

“Driving through the Sonoma hills in January, it always gets a carpet of green grass; it was beautiful. I knew that, and it was just the perfect light, the perfect clouds," he added.

The photo was uploaded to a stock photo agency by the 79-year-old.

When Microsoft discovered O'Rear's shot, it paid an undisclosed sum for perpetual rights to it and immediately plastered it across the globe as part of a $1 billion (KSh 109,770,000,000) marketing campaign.

More than 400 million copies of Windows XP were sold in its first five years. The operating system wasn't retired until 2014, meaning more than a billion people have very likely seen O’Rear’s photo without ever realising it was from the Bay Area.

Despite O'Rear's extensive photography career, which includes work for the Los Angeles Times, The Kansas City Star, and National Geographic for more than two decades, he is well aware that Bliss" Hills image is the only thing he will be remembered for.

He said not a week goes without an email coming through about the photograph.

Despite the ubiquity and fame the image has brought him, having his legacy tied to the technology company did not buy his loyalty.

“I got hooked on Apple,” he said.
